## Red-team Targeted Fixes
### **Key Summary of the Document:**

#### **1. Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICSs):**
- **Effectiveness**: ICSs are the **most effective medications** for long-term control of persistent asthma. They target inflammation in the lungs when used daily.
- **Safety**: 
  - **Side Effects**: Can cause mouth irritation or thrush (preventable by rinsing after use). Growth may slow slightly in children, but this is generally small and not progressive.
  - **Pregnancy**: ICSs are **safe for pregnant women** when used to control asthma.
- **Usage**: Should be used at the **lowest effective dose** to maintain control, combined with proper technique (e.g., spacer devices).

---

#### **2. Long-Acting Beta-2 Agonists (LABAs):**
- **Combination with ICSs**: LABAs (e.g., salmeterol, formoterol) should **never be used alone**. They must be combined with an ICS for long-term asthma control.
- **Risks**: 
  - LABAs increase the risk of severe asthma exacerbations, especially at higher doses.
  - The FDA issued a **Black Box warning** due to rare but serious risks (e.g., deaths in clinical trials for salmeterol).
- **Dosage**: Daily use should not exceed **100 mcg salmeterol** or **24 mcg formoterol**.
- **Usage**: Not recommended for **acute symptoms** or exacerbations.

---

#### **3. Patient Education:**
- **ICSs**: Explain that they are preventive medications (not for quick relief). Emphasize daily use and lowest effective dose.
- **LABAs**: Highlight the need to combine them with ICSs and avoid monotherapy. Stress the importance of monitoring for worsening symptoms.
