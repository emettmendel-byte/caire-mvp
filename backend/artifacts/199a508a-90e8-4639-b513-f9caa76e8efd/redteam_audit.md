## Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS)

1.  **Purpose:** ICSs are the **most effective** medications for the **long-term control** of persistent asthma. They target the lungs directly to reduce chronic airway inflammation.
2.  **Usage:** Should be taken **every day** to prevent asthma symptoms and attacks, even when feeling well. Dosing is based on the lowest effective dose needed for control, determined by the clinician based on patient response (symptoms, activity, lung function).
3.  **Benefits:** Highly effective for control, anti-inflammatory action in the airways.
4.  **Risks/Side Effects:**
    *   **Common:** Potential for mouth irritation and thrush (yeast infection) – can be avoided by rinsing and spitting after use, and using a spacer if available.
    *   **Less Common:** Potential slight slowing of linear growth in children (not predictable, generally small, occurs early, not progressive; clinical significance unclear; poorly controlled asthma itself can slow growth).
    *   **Serious (rare):** Generally well-tolerated, but like all medications, has potential side effects. The benefits usually outweigh the risks when used as directed.
    *   **Safety:** Generally considered safe for pregnant women, as controlling asthma is crucial for fetal oxygenation. Not addictive and not the same as performance-enhancing anabolic steroids.
5.  **Monitoring:** Clinicians must monitor patient response and carefully titrate the dose down to the minimum needed once control is achieved and sustained.

## Long-Acting Beta-2 Agonists (LABA)

1.  **Purpose:** LABAs (e.g., salmeterol, formoterol) are **add-on controllers** used in combination with an inhaled corticosteroid (ICS) for asthma patients who require more than low-dose ICS alone for control. They improve lung function, reduce symptoms, and decrease asthma attacks and short-acting beta-agonist (SABA) use.
2.  **Crucial Combination:** **LABAs should never be used alone** for long-term control. They must always be combined with an ICS.
3.  **Usage:** Daily use should generally not exceed 100 mcg salmeterol or 24 mcg formoterol. Should not be used to treat acute symptoms or sudden worsening (exacerbations).
4.  **Risks/Safety:**
    *   **Black Box Warning:** The FDA issued a Black Box warning due to an increased risk of severe asthma exacerbations (worsening asthma) associated with daily LABA use, although uncommon.
    *   **Benefits vs. Risks:** The proven benefits of LABAs when combined with ICS for most patients needing stronger control must be weighed against the risk of severe exacerbations.
5.  **FDA Warning:** Based on clinical trial data showing increased risks (especially for salmeterol), the FDA mandated a Black Box warning on all LABA-containing medications.
6.  **Patient Education:** Emphasize the need for daily ICS use even when on LABA, the importance of monitoring, and the specific warnings regarding LABA use.

In essence, the document emphasizes the primary role of ICSs for persistent asthma control, the necessity of combining LABAs with ICSs, and provides key points for patient education and safety regarding both drug classes.
