**Document Overview:**
*   This is a guide for healthcare professionals, patients, and families managing persistent asthma.
*   Focuses on medications for **long-term control** of asthma, particularly **inhaled corticosteroids (ICS)** and **long-acting beta-2 agonists (LABAs)**.

**Key Medications & Their Use:**

1.  **Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS):**
    *   **Role:** The **most effective** medication class for long-term asthma control. They target airway inflammation directly.
    *   **Usage:** Should be used **daily**, even when symptoms are well-controlled, to prevent attacks.
    *   **Effectiveness:** Improves lung function, reduces symptoms, and prevents asthma attacks.
    *   **Side Effects:** Generally safe, but common side effects like **mouth irritation** (sores, thrush) can be avoided by rinsing the mouth after use and using a spacer (valved holding chamber) if available. There may be a **slight, generally small, and not progressive effect on growth rate** in children. This effect is not predictable and occurs mainly in the first few months. The clinical significance is still being determined, but poorly controlled asthma itself can slow growth. ICS are **safe during pregnancy**.
    *   **Importance:** Not addictive and not related to anabolic steroids used illegally by athletes.

2.  **Long-Acting Beta-2 Agonists (LABAs):**
    *   **Role:** Bronchodilators that keep airways open for longer (12+ hours).
    *   **Crucial Rule:** **Must be used ONLY in combination with an ICS** for long-term control. They are **not** a substitute for ICS.
    *   **Safety:** Carry a **Black Box Warning** due to an increased risk of severe asthma exacerbations (worsening asthma attacks), although this risk is uncommon. A large study found slightly more deaths in patients using salmeterol (a LABA) daily, and trials showed more severe exacerbations with formoterol (another LABA), especially at higher doses, compared to placebo.
    *   **Usage:** Should not be used to treat acute symptoms or sudden attacks. Daily use should generally not exceed 100 mcg salmeterol or 24 mcg formoterol.

**Patient Education & Management:**

*   **Combination Therapy:** Emphasize using LABA *only* with ICS if needed beyond low-dose ICS alone.
*   **Benefits vs. Risks:** The benefits of LABAs with ICS for most patients needing stronger control should be weighed against the risk of severe exacerbations.
*   **Proper Technique:** Ensure patients understand correct inhaler use (including rinsing for ICS and using spacers).
*   **Addressing Misconceptions:** Clarify that ICS are not addictive and that the growth effect, while noted, is generally minimal and not progressive.
*   **Dosing:** Once asthma is well-controlled, the dose should be reduced to the minimum necessary to maintain control, based on the clinician's judgment and patient response.
