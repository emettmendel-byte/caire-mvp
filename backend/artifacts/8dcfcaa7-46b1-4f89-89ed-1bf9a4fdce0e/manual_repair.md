Here's a concise breakdown of the key points from the asthma care document:

**1. Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICSs):**
*   **Purpose:** Most effective for long-term control of persistent asthma. Target inflammation in the lungs.
*   **Usage:** Daily use is essential for prevention, not just symptom relief.
*   **Benefits:** Proven effectiveness for control.
*   **Risks/Side Effects:**
    *   Potential for mouth irritation/thrush (avoided by rinsing/spitting).
    *   May slightly slow linear growth (generally small, not predictable, not progressive; compare to risks of uncontrolled asthma).
    *   Generally safe during pregnancy.
    *   Not addictive; not the same as performance-enhancing anabolic steroids.

**2. Long-Acting Beta2-Agonists (LABAs):**
*   **Purpose:** Added to asthma treatment (often with ICS) to improve lung function, reduce symptoms, and decrease attacks/exacerbations when needed at doses higher than low-dose ICS alone.
*   **Crucial Points:**
    *   **Combination Therapy:** MUST be used *only* with an inhaled corticosteroid (ICS). Should not be used as monotherapy for long-term control.
    *   **FDA Warning (Black Box):** Due to an increased risk of severe asthma exacerbations (even uncommon), especially with daily use. This risk is highlighted by the FDA.
    *   **Dosage Limit:** Daily use should generally not exceed 100 mcg salmeterol or 24 mcg formoterol.
    *   **Not for Acute Symptoms:** Should not be used to treat sudden asthma symptoms or attacks (reserved for quick-relief SABA medications).
*   **Key Risk:** Uncommon but serious risk of severe asthma worsening (exacerbations) with daily LABA use.

**3. General Dosing & Monitoring:**
*   Dosing depends on the individual's response. Clinicians must monitor symptoms, activity, and lung function to adjust the dose.
*   Once asthma is well-controlled for at least 3 months, the dose should be gradually reduced to the minimum needed to maintain control.

In summary, the document emphasizes the primary role of ICSs in asthma control, the necessity of daily use, and the importance of managing potential side effects. For LABAs, it stresses their specific role as *adjuncts* to ICS, the critical importance of combination therapy, and the associated but rare risk of severe exacerbations, leading to FDA warnings.
