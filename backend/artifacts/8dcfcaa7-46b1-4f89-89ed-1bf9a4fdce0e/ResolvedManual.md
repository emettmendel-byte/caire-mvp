## I. Medications for Long-Term Control (LTC)

1.  **Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS):**
    *   **Role:** First-line treatment for persistent asthma. Most effective for long-term control.
    *   **Mechanism:** Reduce chronic airway inflammation directly in the lungs when inhaled.
    *   **Usage:** Taken daily (every day) to prevent symptoms and attacks, even when feeling well.
    *   **Benefits:** Proven effectiveness in controlling inflammation.
    *   **Risks/Side Effects:**
        *   Generally safe at prescribed doses.
        *   Common side effects (more likely at higher doses): Throat irritation, hoarseness, increased chance of thrush (yeast infection). These can be minimized by rinsing mouth after use and using a spacer device if available.
        *   Potential effect on linear growth rate (slight, small, not predictable, appears early but not progressive). The clinical significance is still being determined, and uncontrolled asthma can also slow growth.
        *   Safe during pregnancy when needed to ensure fetal oxygen supply.
        *   Not addictive; not the same as performance-enhancing anabolic steroids.
    *   **Growth Concerns:** While a slight effect on growth may occur, it is generally small and not progressive. Growth rates vary greatly in children, and poorly controlled asthma can significantly impact growth negatively.

2.  **Long-Acting Beta2-Agonists (LABAs):**
    *   **Role:** *Never used alone* for long-term control. Used *only* in combination with an Inhaled Corticosteroid (ICS).
    *   **Mechanism:** Relax airway muscles for longer periods (hours) than short-acting relievers (SABA).
    *   **Benefits (when combined with ICS):** Can improve lung function, reduce symptoms, and decrease asthma attacks and SABA use more effectively than doubling the ICS dose for many patients who need more than low-dose ICS alone.
    *   **Risks/Side Effects (FDA Black Box Warning):** Increased risk of severe asthma exacerbations (worsening asthma) and, uncommonly, asthma-related deaths, especially with daily use *without* the prescribed ICS. This risk is higher at higher doses.
    *   **Usage:**
        *   **Crucial:** Must be *combined* with an ICS.
        *   **Maximum Daily Dose:** Should generally not exceed 100 mcg salmeterol or 24 mcg formoterol per day.
        *   **Not for Acute Symptoms:** Should not be used to treat sudden asthma symptoms or attacks (use a SABA for relief).
        *   **Not Monotherapy:** Should not be used as the sole controller medication.

## II. Key Considerations

*   **Lowest Effective Dose:** Use the lowest dose of ICS necessary to maintain control to minimize potential side effects.
*   **Correct Technique:** Proper use of inhaler devices (including spacers for young children or those with difficulty) is essential for medication delivery.
*   **Regular Monitoring:** Clinicians must monitor patient response (symptoms, activity, lung function) and adjust doses accordingly. Once control is achieved and sustained, the dose should be gradually reduced.
*   **Combination Therapy:** LABAs are only effective and safe when used *with* an ICS.

## III. Responding to Patient Questions

*   **ICS Questions:** Emphasize effectiveness, daily use, safety (when used correctly and at appropriate doses), and address common myths (not addictive, side effects are manageable). Explain the growth concern but clarify it's usually small and not worse than uncontrolled asthma.
*   **LABA Questions:** Clearly explain the necessity of combining with ICS, the potential risks (especially the Black Box warning), the maximum dose, and that they are not for quick relief.
