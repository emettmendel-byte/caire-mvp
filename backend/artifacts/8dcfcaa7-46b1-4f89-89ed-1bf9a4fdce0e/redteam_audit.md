Okay, let's break down the key points from this asthma care document:

1.  **Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICSs):**
    *   **Primary Use:** Most effective medication class for long-term control of persistent asthma. They target inflammation in the airways.
    *   **Usage:** Must be used daily for effective control, even when symptoms are minimal. They are not primarily for quick symptom relief (that's for Short-Acting Beta2-Agonists/SABA rescue inhalers).
    *   **Benefits:** Proven effectiveness in controlling inflammation and preventing asthma attacks.
    *   **Risks/Side Effects:**
        *   Potential for local side effects like mouth/throat irritation or thrush (sores) if not rinsed after use.
        *   May slightly slow linear growth in children (generally small, not predictable, not progressive; compare to risks of uncontrolled asthma).
        *   Generally considered safe during pregnancy.
        *   Not addictive; not the same as performance-enhancing anabolic steroids.

2.  **Long-Acting Beta2-Agonists (LABAs):**
    *   **Purpose:** Used *only* as a *combination* therapy with an ICS. They help keep airways open for longer periods (12-24 hours) and are more effective than doubling the ICS dose for control in some cases.
    *   **Crucial Points:**
        *   **Combination Therapy:** MUST be used *only* with an ICS. Should never be used alone for long-term control.
        *   **FDA Warning (Black Box):** Due to an increased risk of severe asthma exacerbations (even uncommon) associated with daily use, the FDA placed a Black Box warning on all LABA-containing drugs.
        *   **Dosage Limit:** Daily use should generally not exceed 100 mcg salmeterol or 24 mcg formoterol.
        *   **Not for Acute Symptoms:** Should not be used to treat sudden asthma symptoms or attacks (reserved for quick-relief SABA medications).

3.  **General Dosing & Monitoring:**
    *   Dosing is individualized based on symptoms and response. Clinicians must monitor symptoms, activity, and lung function to adjust the dose.
    *   Once asthma is well-controlled for at least 3 months, the dose should be gradually reduced to the minimum needed to maintain control.

4.  **Educational Resources:** The document lists several reputable organizations and websites (NHLBI, AAP, AARC, etc.) for further information and support.

In summary, the document emphasizes the primary role of ICSs in asthma control, the necessity of daily use, and the importance of managing potential side effects. For LABAs, it stresses their specific role as *adjuncts* to ICS, the critical importance of combination therapy, and the associated but rare risk of severe exacerbations, leading to FDA warnings.
